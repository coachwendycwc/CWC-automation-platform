3-4 minutes

You carved out time in what I know is an incredibly busy schedule to invest in yourself and your career. That matters. That's leadership in action."

You've been carrying a lot—the weight of your work, your ambitions, maybe some frustration about not being where you thought you'd be by now. I want you to set that down for the next two hours. This is your time."

"This month, we're tackling something that trips up so many talented women of color—and honestly, it's one of the most frustrating dynamics in corporate America. It's the gap between doing excellent work and actually being recognized for that work. It's the space between delivering results and getting promoted for those results."

"Today's session is called Strategic Positioning for Promotion. Now, I chose those words very intentionally. This isn't about working harder—you're already doing that. This isn't about being more qualified—many of you are overqualified. This is about being strategic. This is about positioning. This is about making sure that when promotion decisions are being made, your name is not just on the list—it's at the top of the list."

Slide 2: Welcome - Your Work Deserves Recognition

4-5 minutes

Complete Script

"Let me share some numbers with you, and I want you to really sit with these because they're not just statistics—they're our lived experience."

"58% of women of color report feeling overlooked for advancement. Let that sink in. More than half of us—despite our degrees, our expertise, our results—feel invisible when it comes to moving up. And here's the thing: feeling overlooked isn't just a feeling. It's based in reality. The research backs it up."

"Women of color are twice as likely to be passed over for promotion compared to our white counterparts with similar qualifications and performance. Twice as likely. That means if there are two equally qualified candidates—one who looks like us and one who doesn't—statistically, we're getting passed over."

"Now, I'm not sharing these numbers to discourage you. I'm sharing them because I need you to understand something critical: this is not about your performance. If you've been working harder, staying later, delivering more, and still not advancing—the problem is not your work ethic. The problem is not your competence. The problem is a visibility gap, and that's exactly what we're going to close today."

"See, here's what nobody tells us: Excellence is expected. Excellence is the price of admission. But excellence alone does not get you promoted. I wish it did. I wish we lived in a world where the best work automatically got recognized and rewarded. But that's not the world we're operating in. In the real world, the people who get promoted are the people whose excellence is visible to the people who make decisions."

"So today, we're going to stop hoping someone notices us. We're going to stop waiting for our manager to advocate for us. We're going to take control of our own visibility, our own narrative, and our own advancement. Sound good?"

Engagement Prompt: "I want to see some hands—virtual or real. Raise your hand if you've ever had that experience where you delivered exceptional work, maybe even saved a project or a client relationship, and then watched someone else get the credit or the recognition. Put a '1' in the chat if that's ever happened to you."

"Look at all these 1s coming in. You're not alone. This is a shared experience, and today we're going to make sure it stops happening."

Slide 4: The Challenge - Excellence Without Visibility

5-6 minutes

Complete Script

"I want to introduce you to three archetypes. These are patterns I see over and over again with brilliant women of color who are stuck despite their talent. See if any of these sound familiar."

"The first is The Silent Achiever. This is the woman who delivers exceptional results. Her projects come in on time, under budget, exceeding expectations. Her clients love her. Her teammates rely on her. But here's the problem: no one outside her immediate team knows her name. She's a well-kept secret within her own organization."

"The Silent Achiever was taught that good work speaks for itself. She was raised to be humble, to not brag, to let her results do the talking. And in theory, that sounds noble. But in practice? In practice, she's getting passed over for promotions that go to people who are less qualified but more visible. She's watching colleagues who do less get recognized more. And she's frustrated because she's doing everything right—except the one thing that matters for advancement."

"The second archetype is The Untracked Wins. This is the woman who does great work but can't articulate it when it matters. When performance review time comes around, she's scrambling. 'What did I do in Q1? I know I did something important, but I can't remember the details.' Her self-assessment ends up generic and underwhelming—not because her work was generic, but because she didn't capture it in the moment."

"Here's the reality: your manager has ten, fifteen, maybe twenty direct reports. They have their own deliverables, their own stress, their own career to manage. They cannot remember every good thing you've done over the past year. That's not their job. It's yours. If you don't track it, it's like it never happened."

"The third archetype is The Missing Advocates. This is the woman who has a good relationship with her direct manager, but that's where her visibility ends. When promotion decisions are being made—and let me tell you how this actually works—there's usually a calibration meeting. A room full of leaders, discussing who should be promoted, who should get the high rating, who should get the bonus. Your manager is in that room, but so are six or seven other leaders."

"If your manager is the only one who can speak to your value, you're at a disadvantage. Because when another leader says, 'I don't know her work,' or 'I've never heard of her,' your manager has to spend their political capital defending you instead of advocating for you. But if multiple leaders in that room can say, 'Oh yes, I know her work—she delivered that cross-functional project brilliantly,' suddenly you have momentum. You have multiple voices championing you."

"Which of these three resonates with you? The Silent Achiever? The Untracked Wins? The Missing Advocates? Most of us are a combination of all three at different points in our career."

Engagement Prompt: "In the chat, tell me: Which of these three archetypes describes where you are right now? Type 'Silent' if you're the Silent Achiever, 'Untracked' if you struggle with documenting your wins, or 'Advocates' if you know you don't have enough people championing you. You can type more than one if multiple apply."

[Wait for responses, acknowledge patterns]

"I'm seeing a lot of 'Silent' and 'Untracked' in here. That's actually good news because those are both things we can fix today. By the end of this session, you'll have systems in place to address both."

Slide 5: Framework - The Visibility Equation

5-6 minutes

Complete Script

"Now let me give you the framework that's going to change how you think about advancement. I call it The Visibility Equation, and once you understand it, you'll never approach your career the same way again."

"The equation is simple: Performance + Positioning + Promotion Criteria = Advancement."

"Let's break this down."

"Performance is the work you do. It's the results you deliver, the projects you complete, the problems you solve. This is where most of us focus 100% of our energy. And listen—performance matters. You cannot be mediocre and expect to advance. But here's the truth that took me years to learn: performance is necessary but not sufficient. It's the table stakes. It's what gets you in the game, but it's not what wins the game."

"Positioning is who knows about your performance and how they perceive you. This is visibility. This is reputation. This is the story that people tell about you when you're not in the room. You could be the highest performer in your entire organization, but if the people who make decisions don't know that—if they've never heard your name, never seen your work, never been told about your impact—then your performance doesn't count toward your advancement."

"Promotion Criteria is what your organization actually rewards. Every company has explicit and implicit criteria for who gets promoted. The explicit criteria might be documented—competencies, skills, behaviors at the next level. The implicit criteria are the unwritten rules—the types of projects that get noticed, the relationships that matter, the way successful people operate in your culture. If your excellent performance isn't aligned with what your organization values and rewards, you're running in the wrong race."

"Here's where most high performers get stuck: they're doing amazing work—performance is high—but their positioning is weak and they're not aligned to promotion criteria. They're excelling at things that aren't being measured or valued for advancement."

"Today, we're going to address all three parts of this equation. You're going to make sure your performance is being documented. You're going to build your positioning strategy. And you're going to align your work to what actually gets rewarded at your organization."

Reflection Prompt: "Take 30 seconds right now and think about this: On a scale of 1-10, where would you rate yourself on each part of this equation? Performance—how strong is your actual work? Positioning—how well do decision-makers know your work? And Promotion Criteria—how well does your work align with what gets rewarded?"

"If you're like most people, your performance score is high, but your positioning and criteria alignment scores are lower. That gap is exactly what we're going to close."

Slide 6: Deep Dive - Know Your Promotion Criteria

5-6 minutes

Complete Script

"Let's start with promotion criteria, because this is the foundation everything else builds on. Here's a question I want you to honestly answer in your own mind: Do you know—specifically and concretely—what your organization's documented promotion criteria are?"

"I'm not asking if you have a vague sense. I'm asking if you could pull up a document right now that tells you exactly what competencies, skills, and behaviors are required for the next level. If you can't, you're not alone—but it's a problem we need to fix."

"You cannot hit a target you cannot see. If you don't know what 'exceeds expectations' actually means at your company, you're guessing. If you're not sure what leadership values beyond the generic 'be a team player' advice, you're shooting in the dark. And if your goals don't explicitly align with promotion criteria, you might be working incredibly hard on things that don't count toward advancement."

"Here's what I want you to do this week—and I mean this week, not someday. Go find your organization's promotion criteria. It might be in your HR portal, your performance management system, or a competency framework document. If you can't find it, ask your HR business partner or your manager directly."

"And when you ask your manager, use this exact language: 'I want to make sure I'm positioning myself for promotion. Can you help me understand what "promotable" looks like in our organization? What are the specific criteria that are used when making promotion decisions?'"

"Notice what this question does. It signals your ambition—you're telling them you want to be promoted. It asks for specificity—you're not accepting vague answers. And it positions you as someone who is strategic about their career, not just hoping to be noticed."

"Once you have the criteria, I want you to do a gap analysis. Look at each criterion and honestly assess: Am I demonstrating this? Do I have evidence of this? Is this visible to decision-makers? Where are my gaps? That analysis becomes your development plan and your visibility strategy."

Transition: "Once you know what you're aiming for, the next question is: Who needs to see you hitting that target? Let's talk about strategic visibility."

Slide 7: Deep Dive - Strategic Visibility

5-6 minutes

Complete Script

"Strategic visibility is about being seen by the right people, at the right time, for the right work. Let me break down each of those components."

"The right people means decision-makers. Not just your manager—though they matter—but the broader group of leaders who influence your advancement. This includes your skip-level leader (your manager's manager), cross-functional leaders who you work with or could work with, and anyone who has a seat at the table when promotions are discussed."

"The right time means consistently, not just at performance review time. If you're invisible for eleven months and then suddenly start talking about your accomplishments in month twelve, that's not visibility—that's desperation, and people can sense the difference. Strategic visibility is a year-round practice."

"The right work means the accomplishments that align with promotion criteria and business priorities. Not every win is created equal. Some wins are interesting but don't move the needle for your advancement. Other wins directly demonstrate that you're ready for the next level. You want to be visible for the work that positions you for promotion."

"Now, let me share some signs that you might be invisible—and I want you to be really honest with yourself here."

"Sign number one: Decision-makers don't know your name. If your skip-level leader doesn't know who you are, if cross-functional leaders couldn't pick you out of a lineup, you have a visibility problem."

"Sign number two: You're not invited to strategic meetings. When the important conversations are happening, are you in the room? Or are you only hearing about decisions after they've been made?"

"Sign number three: Your manager takes credit for your work—or at minimum, doesn't explicitly attribute it to you. When your manager presents team accomplishments to leadership, do they name you? Do they say, 'This was led by [your name]'? Or does it become 'my team did this' with no individual recognition?"

"Sign number four: Opportunities go to less qualified peers. This is the most painful one. You watch someone with less experience, less skill, or weaker results get the opportunity you wanted. And often, the difference isn't merit—it's visibility. They were known. You weren't."

"The breakthrough here isn't working harder. It's working smarter on your visibility. It's identifying who needs to see your work, finding authentic channels to reach them, sharing wins consistently, and volunteering for high-visibility projects."

Slide 8: Deep Dive - Win Documentation

4-5 minutes

Complete Script

"Here's a truth that might be uncomfortable: If you don't track it, it didn't happen. Not because your work didn't matter, but because you won't have the evidence when you need it."

"Let me paint a picture. It's December. Performance review season. Your manager asks you to write your self-assessment. You sit down to write it, and suddenly your mind goes blank. 'What did I do this year? I know I was busy. I know I was stressed. But what were the actual accomplishments?'"

"So you scramble. You dig through old emails trying to find projects. You look at your calendar trying to remember meetings. You end up with a self-assessment that's generic and underwhelming—phrases like 'supported the team' and 'contributed to projects' instead of specific, measurable impact statements."

"And here's the worst part: your manager reads that self-assessment and thinks, 'Huh, I guess she didn't do that much this year.' Not because you didn't do amazing work—but because you couldn't remember it and articulate it compellingly."

"Meanwhile, your colleague—who maybe did less than you—wrote a detailed self-assessment with specific metrics, named accomplishments, and clear impact. Guess who looks like the higher performer on paper?"

"This is why win documentation is not optional. It's the foundation of everything else we're doing today. You need a system to capture your wins in real-time, while the details are fresh, while you remember the metrics, while you can articulate the impact."

"I'm going to give you a framework in the next slide that makes this easy. It takes five minutes a week. Five minutes. That's it. And those five minutes will transform your performance reviews, your promotion conversations, and your confidence in advocating for yourself."

Slide 10: Root Causes - Why Your Work Goes Unseen

5-6 minutes

Complete Script

"Before we get into solutions, I want to name the root causes of invisibility. Because if we don't understand why this happens, we can't fully address it."

"Root cause number one: Modesty culture. Many of us were raised with messages like 'don't be boastful,' 'let your work speak for itself,' 'pride comes before a fall.' These messages were often delivered with good intentions, but they don't serve us in corporate environments where visibility is currency. We internalized that talking about ourselves is inappropriate, and now we're suffering the consequences professionally."

"Root cause number two: No tracking system. This is practical, not psychological. Without a system to capture wins as they happen, they disappear. You finish a project, you feel good for a day, you move on to the next fire, and three months later you can't remember the details. The win is lost—not because it didn't matter, but because you didn't capture it."

"Root cause number three: Wrong audience. You might be visible to your immediate team. Your manager might know your work. But if the people who make promotion decisions don't know you, your visibility isn't reaching far enough. You're famous in the wrong room."

"Root cause number four: Inconsistent communication. Some people only become visible at performance review time. They're silent for eleven months and then suddenly start trumpeting their accomplishments. This comes across as desperate and inauthentic. Effective visibility is consistent—regular, predictable, ongoing."

"Root cause number five: Fear of being seen as 'too much.' Especially for women of color, there's a real fear of being perceived as aggressive, pushy, or self-aggrandizing. We've been told—sometimes explicitly, sometimes subtly—to not take up too much space. So we shrink. We downplay. We attribute our success to the team or to luck instead of owning our individual contribution."

"Root cause number six: Waiting to be noticed. This is the most insidious one. Deep down, many of us believe that if we just work hard enough, someone will eventually see us and reward us. We're waiting for a fairy godmother manager to discover our brilliance and elevate us. But that's not how organizations work. The people who get promoted are the people who take ownership of their visibility—they don't wait to be discovered."

"Which of these root causes resonates most for you? I want you to identify your primary barrier, because awareness is the first step to change."

Slide 12: Framework - The Win-Tracking System

6-7 minutes

Complete Script

"Now let me give you the framework that makes win documentation simple and powerful. It's called What / So What / Now What, and every single win you capture should answer these three questions."

"WHAT is the accomplishment itself. What did you do? Be specific about the deliverable, the project, the action you completed. Don't be vague here—'Supported the marketing campaign' tells me nothing. 'Led the email strategy for the Q3 product launch, including A/B testing and segmentation' tells me exactly what you did."

"SO WHAT is the business impact. Why does this matter? This is where you quantify with metrics wherever possible. Revenue generated, costs saved, time reduced, problems solved, customers retained, efficiency improved. If you can put a number on it, put a number on it. If you can't, describe the qualitative impact—what changed as a result of your work?"

"NOW WHAT is the connection to something larger. This win positions you for what? How does it connect to future goals, larger initiatives, or promotion criteria? This is where you show strategic thinking. You're not just completing tasks—you're driving toward something bigger."

"Let me give you two examples to make this concrete."

"Corporate example: 'WHAT: I led the Q1 product launch, managing a cross-functional team of 12 people across engineering, design, and marketing. SO WHAT: The launch generated $2 million in new revenue in the first 90 days and increased our customer base by 15%, exceeding our target by 40%. NOW WHAT: This positions our product line for the enterprise market expansion, which is a key strategic priority for the business this year—and demonstrates my readiness for a senior leadership role managing larger, more complex initiatives.'"

"Non-profit example: 'WHAT: I redesigned our volunteer onboarding program, creating new training materials, a buddy system, and a 30-day check-in process. SO WHAT: This increased volunteer retention by 40% and reduced the time from application to active volunteering by two weeks. NOW WHAT: This enables us to scale our community outreach initiative ahead of schedule and positions me as someone who can design and implement programs that drive organizational impact.'"

"Do you see how different this is from 'I worked on the product launch' or 'I helped with volunteer training'? The What/So What/Now What framework forces you to be specific, quantify impact, and connect to strategy."

Slide 13: Activity - Start Your Win Log

"A few tips as you work:"

Slide 14: Visibility Strategy Checklist

4-5 minutes

Complete Script

"Now that you have your wins documented, let's talk about how to strategically share them. This is the Visibility Strategy Checklist—your six-step system for making sure your work gets seen by the right people."

"Step 1: Promotion Criteria. Document what your organization rewards. We talked about this earlier—you need to know the rules of the game. What competencies are required at the next level? What behaviors are valued? This is your target."

"Step 2: Key Stakeholders. Identify 3-5 decision-makers who need to know your work. This always includes your manager, but it should also include your skip-level leader and at least one cross-functional leader. These are the people who will influence your advancement."

"Step 3: Communication Channels. How will you reach each stakeholder? This looks different for different relationships. With your manager, it might be your regular 1:1s. With your skip-level, it might be occasional project updates or asking to present at their team meeting. With cross-functional leaders, it might be collaborative projects or internal networking. Map out the channel for each person."

"Step 4: Frequency. How often will you share wins with each stakeholder? Your manager might hear about wins weekly. Your skip-level might get a monthly update. Cross-functional leaders might hear from you quarterly or project-by-project. The key is consistency—you want to be on their radar regularly, not just when you need something."

"Step 5: Win Categories. Not all wins are equal for visibility purposes. Which types of wins should you highlight? Focus on wins that align with promotion criteria, demonstrate strategic impact, and are relevant to each stakeholder's priorities. Tailor your message."

"Step 6: Review Rhythm. Set a monthly check-in with yourself to assess your visibility. Are you reaching your target stakeholders? Are you sharing wins consistently? What's working? What needs adjustment? This keeps you accountable."

Slide 16: Performance Management Cycle

5-6 minutes

Complete Script

"Let's talk about something that most people get completely wrong: performance reviews. The way most people approach reviews is reactive. They wait until review season, scramble to remember what they did, write a rushed self-assessment, and hope for the best."

"I want you to think about it differently. Your performance review is not an annual event—it's a year-long campaign. The outcome is determined by what you do in the months leading up to it, not what you do the week before."

"Let me walk you through how to own each phase of the performance cycle."

"At Goal Setting: This is where most people miss their first opportunity. When you set your goals for the year, you should explicitly align them to promotion criteria. Look at what's required for the next level, and make sure your goals give you opportunities to demonstrate those competencies. Don't just accept whatever goals your manager suggests—negotiate for goals that position you for advancement."

"Throughout the Year: This is where your win tracking system comes in. You're not waiting until review time to document your accomplishments. You're capturing them in real-time, every week, using the What/So What/Now What framework. By review time, you'll have 50 weeks of documented wins to draw from."

"At Mid-Cycle Review: Don't wait for your manager to offer a mid-cycle check-in. Request one proactively. This is your opportunity to course-correct early. Ask for feedback: 'How am I tracking toward my goals? What should I adjust? What would make the difference between 'meets expectations' and 'exceeds expectations' in the second half of the year?'"

"For Your Self-Assessment: This is your chance to make your case in writing. Don't be modest. Don't be generic. Write your self-assessment like it's a promotion packet. Lead with impact and metrics. Connect your accomplishments to business outcomes and promotion criteria. Make it easy for your manager to advocate for you."

"Before Calibration: In most organizations, after managers write reviews, there's a calibration meeting where leaders discuss ratings across the team. Your manager will be in that room advocating for you—or not. Your job is to arm them with specific examples and talking points they can use. Send them a summary of your top accomplishments before calibration. Make their job easy."

Slide 17: Maximize Your Check-Ins

5-6 minutes

Complete Script

"Now let's talk about your regular check-ins with your manager. Most people use these meetings for status updates and problem-solving. That's fine, but it's a missed opportunity. Every check-in is a visibility opportunity."

"I want to give you a formula that transforms your check-ins: Win + Ask."

"The Win component: Open every check-in by sharing a recent accomplishment with impact. Not 'I've been busy this week.' Instead: 'I completed the client analysis ahead of schedule, and it identified $500K in cost savings they hadn't seen before—they were thrilled.' You're making sure your manager hears about your wins regularly, not just when you remember to mention them."

"The Ask component: Follow your win with a strategic ask. This could be asking for a growth opportunity, asking for visibility, or asking for feedback. 'Given the success of that analysis, I'd like to take on the Q3 strategic planning project to build more experience with executive presentations.' You're signaling ambition and driving your own development."

"Now let me give you three power questions that you should weave into your check-ins throughout the year."

"Question 1: 'What would make me promotable in the next cycle?' This is direct and powerful. You're asking for explicit criteria. The answer gives you a roadmap."

"Question 2: 'Who else should know about this work?' When you share a win, ask this follow-up. You're enlisting your manager's help in expanding your visibility. Maybe they'll CC someone important on an email. Maybe they'll mention you in a leadership meeting. You're making it easy for them to amplify you."

"Question 3: 'Are there stretch opportunities I should be pursuing?' This signals that you're ready for more. You're not waiting to be assigned—you're asking for challenges. It positions you as someone who is ambitious and growth-oriented."

Slide 18: Check-In Prep Template

4-5 minutes

Complete Script

"I'm going to give you a tool that will transform how prepared you are for every manager conversation. It's called the Check-In Prep Template, and it takes just 5 minutes to complete before each meeting."

"Section 1: Wins This Period. List 2-3 accomplishments with measurable impact since your last check-in. These become your conversation starters. You'll never again walk into a meeting without something concrete to share."

"Section 2: Visibility Ask. Identify one opportunity or introduction you want to request. Maybe you want to present at a leadership meeting. Maybe you want to be introduced to a senior leader. Maybe you want to be considered for a high-visibility project. Write it down so you don't forget to ask."

"Section 3: Career Signal. Clarify what you want to be known for. This keeps your long-term positioning in mind, even during tactical conversations. Are you trying to be seen as a strategic thinker? A people developer? A problem-solver? Make sure your check-in reinforces that brand."

"Section 4: Feedback Request. Come with a specific question for your manager. Not 'How am I doing?' which invites a vague response. Instead: 'How did you feel about my handling of the client escalation last week? What would you have done differently?' Specific questions get useful answers."

"Section 5: Next Commitments. Document what you'll deliver by the next check-in. This creates accountability and shows you're forward-thinking."

"I'm sharing this template in the chat. Use it before your next 1:1. I guarantee your manager will notice the difference in how prepared and strategic you are."

Slide 19: Your Sponsor Pipeline

5-6 minutes

Complete Script

"Let's talk about sponsors. Not mentors—sponsors. There's a critical difference."

"A mentor gives you advice. They talk to you about your career. A sponsor gives you opportunities. They talk about you when you're not in the room. Sponsors put their reputation on the line to advocate for your advancement. They use their political capital on your behalf."

"You need to build a sponsor pipeline—a set of leaders who are consistently aware of your work and willing to champion you. I recommend starting with three people."

"Person 1: Your Direct Manager. This is your first line of advocacy. Your manager needs to have detailed, specific evidence of your accomplishments so they can fight for you in calibration meetings and promotion discussions. Don't assume they remember everything—keep them informed. Make it easy for them to advocate by giving them the talking points."

"Person 2: Your Skip-Level Leader. This is your manager's manager. They have significantly more influence in promotion decisions than your direct manager alone. In many organizations, the skip-level has to approve promotions. If they don't know your work, your manager has to spend their political capital educating them—which is less effective than if the skip-level already knows you."

"Person 3: A Cross-Functional Leader. This is someone outside your direct reporting line who can speak to your impact across the organization. When promotion discussions happen, having someone from a different function say 'I've worked with her and she's exceptional' carries enormous weight. It shows your value extends beyond your immediate team."

"Your goal this month—and I want you to commit to this—is to make sure each of these three people knows at least one recent win of yours. That might mean sending your manager a quick summary. It might mean finding an opportunity to update your skip-level. It might mean scheduling a coffee chat with a cross-functional leader."

"Three people. One win each. That's achievable, and it starts building your sponsor pipeline."

Slide 22: Summary - Key Takeaways

4-5 minutes

Complete Script

"Takeaway 2: Track wins in real-time. If you don't document your accomplishments, they functionally didn't happen. You can't advocate for yourself with vague memories. You need specific, quantified evidence. Your win tracker is your career receipt book—maintain it religiously."

"Takeaway 3: Own your performance conversations. Every check-in, every review, every conversation with your manager is a visibility opportunity. Stop treating them as administrative obligations. Start treating them as strategic career moments."

"Takeaway 5: Consistency beats one-time efforts. Visibility isn't built in a day or during review season. It's built through small, regular actions over time. A monthly update to your skip-level. A weekly win shared with your manager. Quarterly touchpoints with cross-functional stakeholders. These small actions compound into career momentum."
