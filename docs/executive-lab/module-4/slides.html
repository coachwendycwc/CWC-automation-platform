Emotional Intelligence
Deep Dive

Emotional Intelligence:
Your Leadership Foundation

EI explains roughly 67% of a leader’s effectiveness. It’s not a personality trait—it’s a skill set you can build.

Signs You Need to Build Your EI

  • You react before you can think—then regret it
  • You shut down completely when triggered
  • You absorb other people’s emotions and carry them home
  • You avoid conflict even when it costs you

The Breakthrough

  • Name what you’re feeling in real-time (emotional literacy)
  • Create space between stimulus and response
  • Read the room without losing yourself in it
  • Choose your response based on strategy, not survival

Source: Frontiers in Psychology, “The Emotional Recession” (2025) · Six Seconds, State of the Heart (2024)

Triggers
Deep Dive

Your Triggers Are Data,
Not Weakness

A trigger is any situation that activates a strong emotional response before your rational brain catches up. Knowing yours is a strategic advantage.

Common Trigger Patterns

  • Being interrupted, spoken over, or having ideas credited to others
  • Tone policing—“You seem upset” when you’re simply direct
  • Being excluded from decisions that affect your work
  • Feeling invisible in rooms where you should be leading

The Breakthrough

  • Map your triggers before they happen
  • Identify the body signal that arrives first (jaw, chest, stomach)
  • Recognize the automatic thought (“They don’t respect me”)
  • Design a chosen response in advance
Microaggressions
Deep Dive

Microaggressions:
The Invisible Drain

Women who experience three or more microaggressions are 4.2 times more likely to feel burned out. These aren’t minor—they’re cumulative and corrosive.

What WOC Face Most

  • “You’re so articulate”—surprise at your competence
  • Being confused with the only other person of color in the room
  • Hair touching, name mispronunciation, cultural assumptions
  • “You’re not like other [group]”—othering disguised as a compliment

Strategic Response Options

  • Address now: When safety and stakes allow it
  • Address later: When timing or audience requires privacy
  • Document: When patterns need evidence for escalation
  • Release: When your energy is better spent elsewhere

Source: LeanIn.Org, State of Black Women in Corporate America (2024) · Derald Wing Sue, Columbia University (2010)

Why High-Stakes Moments
Hit Harder for WOC

Stereotype Threat

The awareness that your reaction will be filtered through racial stereotypes adds a layer of cognitive load that others don’t carry

Hypervisibility

Being “the only” means every reaction is noticed, remembered, and generalized to your entire group

The Emotional Tax

58% of Black women report feeling perpetually “on guard” at work—a constant state of heightened alertness (Catalyst, 2018)

Code-Switching Fatigue

64% of Latinas say they feel pressure to adjust their communication style or personality at work to avoid being stereotyped (McKinsey & LeanIn.Org, 2023)

Racial Trauma Load

57% of Latina adults report experiencing discrimination based on their ethnicity—a burden of chronic stress carried into every professional setting (Harvard School of Public Health / NPR / RWJF, 2017)

Isolation

Without peers who share your experience, there’s no safe space to process what just happened before moving to the next meeting

Tool

The Response Continuum

Not every moment requires the same response. Knowing when to address something is as important as knowing how.

Address Now

When: You have safety, the stakes are high, and silence would be interpreted as agreement.

How: Name what happened clearly and calmly. “I want to flag something. When you said X, here’s the impact.”

Address Later

When: The audience is wrong, emotions are too hot, or you need time to choose your words.

How: Follow up 1-on-1 within 48 hours. “I wanted to circle back on something from yesterday’s meeting.”

Document & Release

When: The cost of addressing outweighs the benefit, but you need a record for patterns.

How: Log it in your Incident Documentation. Release the emotional charge through your Composure Toolkit.

Microaggression Response Scripts

Four strategic response patterns. Each one works differently depending on the situation, the person, and your goal.

Ask

Invite them to examine what they said.

“Can you say more about what you mean by that?”
“I’m curious what led you to that assumption.”

Name

State the impact directly without attacking.

“That comment landed as [impact]. I want to name that.”
“I don’t think that was your intent, but here’s how it felt.”

Redirect

Steer the conversation back to substance.

“Let’s refocus on the proposal I was presenting.”
“I’d like to get back to the data.”

Boundary

Set a clear limit when the line has been crossed.

“That’s not something I’m willing to discuss.”
“I need you to stop. That’s not appropriate.”

Activity
Practice

Practice: Microaggression Scenarios

Rehearsal builds muscle memory. Practice these scenarios so your response is ready when the moment arrives.

Scenario 1: The Credit Thief

In a meeting, a colleague restates your idea and receives praise for it. Your manager nods along without acknowledging you.
Your move: Use Ask or Redirect to reclaim your contribution without escalating.

Scenario 2: The Tone Police

“I’ve gotten some feedback that your communication style can be a lot. You might want to think about your delivery.” —No specific incident. No examples. Just coded language.
Your move: Use Ask to make the vagueness visible: “Can you be specific? What behavior are you referring to?”

Scenario 3: The Surprise Compliment

“Wow, you’re so articulate! Where did you go to school?”
Your move: Use Ask to surface the assumption. Or Redirect to move past it with your dignity intact.

Scenario 4: The Exclusion

You discover a decision about your project was made in a meeting you weren’t invited to. Again.
Your move: Use Boundary with your manager. Document the pattern. Escalate if it continues.

The Composure Toolkit

Four evidence-based practices you can use in real-time—in meetings, on calls, or before difficult conversations.

Box Breathing

Inhale 4 seconds → Hold 4 seconds → Exhale 4 seconds → Hold 4 seconds. Repeat 3–4 cycles. Activates your parasympathetic nervous system in under 60 seconds.

5-4-3-2-1 Grounding

Name 5 things you see, 4 you hear, 3 you can touch, 2 you smell, 1 you taste. Pulls you out of the emotional spiral and into the present moment.

The Power Pause

When triggered: (1) Take a breath. (2) Drop your shoulders. (3) Unclench your jaw. Three physical resets that buy you 5–10 seconds of decision-making space.

The Strategic Exit

“I want to give this the attention it deserves. Let me come back to you on this.” A professional way to remove yourself when you need space to respond, not react.
