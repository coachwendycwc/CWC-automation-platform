The Challenge

The Pivot Paradox:
Qualified But Questioned

You have the skills. You have the track record. But when you try to move into something new, people see a gap instead of a bridge.

The Expertise Trap

You're so associated with your current role that people can't see you doing anything else—even when your skills clearly transfer.

The Prove-It-Again Tax

WOC already face higher standards. A pivot amplifies this—you're asked to prove competence that's already been demonstrated.

The Story Gap

Without a clear narrative connecting past to future, hiring managers fill in the blanks with doubt instead of confidence.

Module 3
WOC Focus

Navigating the
"Prove It Again" Bias

When WOC pivot, they face a double standard: proving they're qualified for the new role while fighting assumptions about why they left the old one.

The Assumption Gap

People assume your pivot means something went wrong, rather than recognizing it as a strategic career move

The Credibility Reset

Years of expertise get mentally discounted because you're entering a new space—this hits WOC hardest

The Network Penalty

Your existing network may not extend into your target space, and cold outreach feels higher-stakes

The Confidence Tax

Internalized doubt compounds external skepticism, making you undersell the very skills that make you valuable

Strategy

Own Your Narrative
Before Someone Else Does

If you don't tell your pivot story, other people will make one up. And their version won't serve you.

What Others Might Assume

  • "She couldn't cut it in her old field"
  • "She's starting from scratch"
  • "She doesn't have relevant experience"
  • "She'll be a flight risk here too"

What Your Story Should Say

  • "I'm bringing proven skills to a new challenge"
  • "This is a strategic, intentional move"
  • "My cross-sector experience is an asset"
  • "I've done the research and I'm all in"
Module 3
Framework

The Pivot Story Script

Every effective pivot story has four parts. This is the structure that makes hiring managers lean in instead of lean back.

1

The Hook

Open with what excites you about the new direction—lead with passion, not apology

2

The Bridge

Connect your past experience to the new role using transferable skills and concrete results

3

The Evidence

Share 2-3 specific accomplishments that prove you can deliver in this new context

4

The Vision

Articulate what you'll bring and why your unique perspective matters in this space

Module 3
Examples

Pivot Stories in Action

Nonprofit → Corporate

Hook: "I'm passionate about scaling impact through business strategy."
Bridge: "Managing a $5M program with 50+ stakeholders taught me operations at scale."
Evidence: "I increased efficiency by 40% and built partnerships across sectors."
Vision: "I bring a stakeholder-first lens most corporate leaders are missing."

Corporate → Government

Hook: "I want to use my strategy skills to drive public impact."
Bridge: "10 years leading cross-functional teams in complex environments."
Evidence: "I delivered a $12M transformation program on time and under budget."
Vision: "Private-sector rigor applied to public-sector missions."

Government → Tech

Hook: "I've been solving complex systems problems—now I want to build the solutions."
Bridge: "Policy design is product thinking at a massive scale."
Evidence: "I redesigned a system serving 2M constituents, reducing wait times by 60%."
Vision: "I bring user empathy that most tech teams lack."

Strategic Outreach

The Informational Interview

The most underrated career pivot tool. It's not about asking for a job—it's about building knowledge and relationships in your target space.

Why They Work

You get insider information, build advocates, and practice your pivot story in a low-stakes environment.

Who to Target

People 1-2 levels above your target role, career pivoters who've done what you want to do, and industry connectors.

How to Ask

Be specific, be respectful of their time, and make it easy to say yes. Lead with admiration, not desperation.

Module 3
Tool

The Outreach Formula

A simple, repeatable approach for reaching out to people in your target space.

The Outreach Script

Compliment: "I admire your work in [specific area]"

Context: "I'm transitioning from [current field] to [target field]"

Ask: "Would you have 20 minutes for a brief conversation?"

Easy Out: "No pressure at all—I appreciate your time either way"

For WOC: You don't need to over-explain your pivot. A confident, concise outreach message signals that you know your worth.

Activity
Practice

Role-Play: The Pivot Conversation

Confidence comes from rehearsal, not wishful thinking. Practice handling the hardest pivot questions before they come up for real.

Scenario 1: The Skeptic

"Why would you leave [current field]? That seems like a big risk."

Your move: Lead with your vision, not your frustration. Reframe risk as strategic growth.

Scenario 2: The Gatekeeper

"We usually look for candidates with direct experience in [target field]."

Your move: Bridge your transferable skills with specific, measurable evidence.

Scenario 3: The Networking Event

"So what do you do?" (and your answer needs to signal your pivot direction)

Your move: Use a forward-looking introduction that positions where you're going, not just where you've been.

Scenario 4: The Interview

"Walk me through your career trajectory and why you're making this change."

Your move: Use the full four-part Pivot Story Script with polished delivery.
