The Challenge

The Pivot Paradox:
Qualified But Questioned

You have the skills. You have the track record. But when you try to move into something new, people see a gap instead of a bridge.

The Expertise Trap

You're so associated with your current role that people can't see you doing anything else—even when your skills clearly transfer.

The Prove-It-Again Tax

WOC already face higher standards. A pivot amplifies this—you're asked to prove competence that's already been demonstrated.

The Story Gap

Without a clear narrative connecting past to future, hiring managers fill in the blanks with doubt instead of confidence.

Transferable Skills
Deep Dive

Skills Travel.
Job Titles Don't.

The skills that made you successful in your current role are the same ones your target role needs. You just need to name them differently.

What You Might Think

  • "I've only ever worked in nonprofits. Corporate won't take me seriously."
  • "I've been in government my whole career. The private sector moves too fast."
  • "I don't have the technical skills for a new industry."

What's Actually True

  • You've managed multi-million-dollar programs with a fraction of corporate budgets—that's an asset, not a limitation.
  • You've navigated one of the most complex, regulation-heavy environments that exists. Speed is easy to learn; operating within complexity is rare.
  • Technical skills are learned in weeks. Leadership, strategic thinking, and stakeholder management take years—and you already have them.

WOC Focus

Navigating the
"Prove It Again" Bias

When WOC pivot, they face a double standard: proving they're qualified for the new role while fighting assumptions about why they left the old one.

The Assumption Gap

People assume your pivot means something went wrong, rather than recognizing it as a strategic career move

The Credibility Reset

Years of expertise get mentally discounted because you're entering a new space—this hits WOC hardest

The Network Penalty

Your existing network may not extend into your target space, and cold outreach feels higher-stakes

The Confidence Tax

Internalized doubt compounds external skepticism, making you undersell the very skills that make you valuable

Module 3
Strategy

Own Your Narrative
Before Someone Else Does

If you don't tell your pivot story, other people will make one up. And their version won't serve you.

What Others Might Assume

  • "She's running away from something"
  • "She'll have to start at the bottom"
  • "Her background doesn't fit"

What Your Story Should Say

  • "I'm running toward something—strategically applying my skills where they create the most impact"
  • "I bring years of leadership experience that directly translates. I'm not starting over—I'm building on what I've accomplished."
  • "My different background is an asset—I bring fresh thinking, diverse experience, and a track record of results."
Module 3
Framework

The Pivot Story Script

Every effective pivot story has four parts. This is the structure that makes hiring managers lean in instead of lean back.

1

The Hook

Open with what excites you about the new direction—lead with passion, not apology

2

The Bridge

Connect your past experience to the new role using transferable skills and concrete results

3

The Evidence

Share 2-3 specific accomplishments that prove you can deliver in this new context

4

The Vision

Articulate what you'll bring and why your unique perspective matters in this space

Module 3
Examples

Pivot Stories in Action

Nonprofit → Corporate

Hook: "How do you create programs that actually change lives? I've spent 15 years answering that question."
Bridge: "Led a team of 30, managed a portfolio serving 50K students annually."
Evidence: "Built data infrastructure, negotiated 12 district partnerships, grew impact by 60%."
Vision: "Ed-tech is asking the same question I've been answering—with different tools."

Corporate → Government

Hook: "I've spent my career analyzing what makes organizations grow. Now I want to help communities grow."
Bridge: "Led financial planning for a $3B business unit across 4 new markets."
Evidence: "Built forecasting models that informed major expansion decisions."
Vision: "Same analytical rigor, bigger impact than shareholder returns."

Government → Tech

Hook: "I've spent a decade navigating federal health programs—now I want to help build the solutions."
Bridge: "Managed a program serving 2M beneficiaries, coordinated across 7 agencies."
Evidence: "Delivered technology implementations under rigorous compliance requirements."
Vision: "I've seen from the inside what healthcare needs—I want to help build it."

Strategic Outreach

The Informational Interview

The most underrated career pivot tool. It's not about asking for a job—it's about building knowledge and relationships in your target space.

Why They Work

You get insider information, build advocates, and practice your pivot story in a low-stakes environment.

Who to Target

People currently in the role you want, people who have successfully pivoted into your target field, and hiring managers or recruiters in your target industry.

How to Ask

Be clear, brief, and easy to say yes to. People generally want to help—approach with genuine curiosity and respect for their time.

Module 3
Tool

The Outreach Formula

A simple, repeatable approach for reaching out to people in your target space.

The Outreach Script

Compliment: Genuine and specific—show you've done your homework

Context: Who you are and why you're reaching out—brief, no apologies

Ask: Specific and time-bounded—"Would you be open to a 20-minute conversation?"

Easy Out: Low-pressure close that actually increases response rates

Example: "Hi Sarah, I recently listened to your panel at the Women in Tech summit and was struck by your insights on building inclusive product teams. I'm a senior leader with twelve years in government program management, exploring a transition into product leadership in health-tech. Would you be open to a brief 20-minute conversation? I completely understand if timing doesn't work."

Tool

Power Questions for
Informational Interviews

1

"What skills or experiences surprised you as being most valuable in this role, that you wouldn't have expected coming in?"

Reveals hidden requirements and transferable skills you didn't know were relevant

2

"If you were making this transition today, knowing what you know now, what would you do differently?"

A shortcut—learn from their mistakes without making them yourself

3

"What concerns might a hiring manager have about someone with my background, and how would you suggest I address them?"

Direct objection handling—tells you exactly what to anticipate and how to frame your story

4

"Is there anyone else you'd recommend I speak with as I explore this transition?"

How one informational interview becomes three—expands your network exponentially

Practice

Role-Play: The Pivot Conversation

Confidence comes from rehearsal, not wishful thinking. Practice handling the hardest pivot questions before they come up for real.

Scenario 1: The Skeptic

"I see you've spent your entire career in the nonprofit sector. This is a corporate environment. What makes you think you can make this transition?"
Your move: Deliver your pivot story with confidence and evidence. Don't get defensive—don't apologize for where you come from.

Scenario 2: The Gatekeeper

"We're really looking for someone with industry experience. I'm not sure your background is the right fit."
Your move: Acknowledge their concern without accepting it. Turn the objection into an opportunity—show what a traditional candidate doesn't bring.

Scenario 3: The Networking Event

"So what do you do?"
Your move: Deliver a 30-second version—Hook + brief Vision. Position where you're going, not just where you've been.

Scenario 4: The Interview

"Walk me through your career and tell me why you're interested in this role."
Your move: Deliver the full four-part Pivot Story: Hook, Bridge, Evidence, Vision. Two minutes, conversational, end with energy.
