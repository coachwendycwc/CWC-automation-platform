Objection Response Cards

Every objection has a response. Know yours before you're in the room.

How to use these cards

Read each objection and response aloud — not silently. The goal is to hear your voice saying the words before you're in the meeting. Highlight the 2 objections most likely to come up for you and practice those responses until they feel natural.

The Six Objections — and Your Responses

Objection 1
"The budget is frozen."
Also: "There's no budget right now." / "We're in a freeze."
Your Response
"I understand the budget constraints. Can we do two things — document this conversation and agree on a timeline to revisit it, say 90 days? And can we also explore non-cash elements: title, expanded scope, additional PTO, or development budget?"
Principle: Expand the table. A budget freeze on salary doesn't mean a freeze on everything.
Objection 2
"You're not quite ready yet."
Also: "Let's give it more time." / "You're almost there."
Your Response
"I appreciate you being honest. Can you help me understand that more specifically — what does 'ready' look like in your view? I'd like clear milestones so I can demonstrate them. And can we schedule a 90-day check-in?"
Principle: Convert vague into accountable. "Not yet" without milestones is a slow no.
Objection 3
"You're at the top of the band."
Also: "The pay band doesn't allow for that." / "You've maxed the level."
Your Response
"I understand. If I'm at the top of the current band, I'd like to understand what the next band looks like and what the path requires. Is a title change part of that conversation? I want to see the full picture."
Principle: The ceiling of one band is the floor of the next. Ask what's above it.
Objection 4
"We can only do the standard increase."
Also: "The process is set." / "Everyone gets the same percentage."
Your Response
"I appreciate the standard process — I'm not asking to skip it. I'm asking to make sure it accounts for impact. Over the past year I [top win] and [second win]. Is there a way to flag this for a separate review?"
Principle: Standard processes aren't designed for above-standard performance. Name your impact.
Objection 5
"I'll need to think about it."
Also: "Let me check with HR." / "I need to loop someone in."
Your Response
"Of course — I appreciate you taking this seriously. Can we set a specific date to reconnect? I want to make sure this conversation keeps its momentum. Would [specific date] work for a follow-up?"
Principle: "I'll think about it" without a timeline is a soft no. Anchor the next conversation before leaving.
Objection 6
"Others here make less than you."
Also: "Your colleagues in similar roles aren't asking for this."
Your Response
"I can only speak to my own experience and contributions. My ask is based on market data and the impact I've delivered. I'd rather keep this focused on what I've contributed and what the market supports. Does that work?"
Principle: Never let them redirect to peers. Your ask is about you. Return to your evidence.

5 Rules for Handling Objections

1

Do not apologize for asking

"I'm sorry, I just thought..." undermines everything that follows. You made a well-researched ask. Nothing to apologize for.

2

Never leave vague on any objection

"Not ready yet" without milestones is a slow no. Convert every vague response into something specific before the conversation ends.

3

You don't have to accept on the spot

"I appreciate that — let me think about it and follow up." Taking time to consider a counter is professional, not weak.

4

Follow up in writing after every negotiation conversation

A brief email summarizing what was discussed and agreed. This protects you and creates accountability regardless of outcome.

5

A flat "no" with no path forward is information

An org that can't provide a clear reason for declining a well-prepared ask — and offers no alternative path — is telling you something about the ceiling in that space.
