Objection Response Cards

Every objection has a response. Know yours before you're in the room.

The ask you can't respond to is the ask you haven't prepared for. Most people crumble not because their case is weak — but because the pushback feels personal and they haven't rehearsed their response. These cards give you the language. Your job is to make it yours.
Objection 1
"The budget is frozen."
Or: "There's no budget right now." / "We're in a hiring freeze."
Your Response
"I understand the budget constraints, and I appreciate you being direct. Can we do two things? First, can we document this conversation and agree on a timeline to revisit it — I'm thinking 90 days. Second, I'd like to explore whether there are non-cash elements we can address now — title, expanded scope, additional PTO, or a professional development budget."
The principle: You are not accepting no. You are managing the timeline and expanding the table. A budget freeze on salary does not mean a freeze on everything.
Objection 2
"You're not quite ready yet."
Or: "Let's give it more time." / "You're almost there."
Your Response
"I appreciate you being honest with me. I'd like to understand that more specifically — can you help me define what 'ready' looks like in your view? I want to be clear on the milestones so I can demonstrate them. And can we schedule a 90-day check-in so I can show you where I am?"
The principle: "Not ready" must never stay vague. You are converting vague feedback into a concrete roadmap — for both of you. Vagueness is how "not yet" becomes never.
Objection 3
"You're at the top of the band."
Or: "The pay band doesn't allow for that." / "You've maxed out this level."
Your Response
"I understand. If I'm at the top of the current band, I'd like to understand what the next band looks like and what the path to that level requires. Is a title change part of that conversation? I want to understand the full picture so I know where to invest my energy."
The principle: The ceiling of one band is the floor of the next. You are not accepting the ceiling — you are asking what is above it.
Objection 4
"We can only do the standard increase."
Or: "The process is set." / "Everyone gets the same percentage."
Your Response
"I appreciate the standard process — I'm not asking to skip it. I'm asking to make sure the process accounts for impact. Over the past year I [name your top win] and [name your second win]. I believe that warrants a conversation outside the standard cycle. Is there a way to flag this for a separate review?"
The principle: You are not asking to break the rules — you are asking to be seen within them. Standard processes are not designed for above-standard performance.
Objection 5
"I'll need to think about it."
Or: "Let me check with [someone]." / "I need to loop in HR."
Your Response
"Of course — I appreciate you taking this seriously. Can we set a specific date to reconnect? I want to make sure this conversation doesn't lose momentum. Would [specific date] work for a follow-up?"
The principle: "I'll think about it" without a timeline is a soft no. You are anchoring the next conversation before you leave this one.
Objection 6
"Other people here make less than you."
Or: "Your colleagues in similar roles aren't asking for this."
Your Response
"I can only speak to my own experience and my own contributions. My ask is based on market data for this role and the impact I've delivered. I'd rather keep this conversation focused on what I've contributed and what the market supports. Does that work?"
The principle: Never let them redirect to peers. Your ask is about you. Comparisons to others are designed to undermine your confidence. Return to your evidence.
Quick-Reference: Objection → Reframe
What They Say What It Usually Means Your Strategic Move
"Budget is frozen" They may genuinely have constraints, or they're testing your resolve Expand the table — non-cash + timeline
"Not ready yet" No clear definition of ready — or actual gaps they haven't named Make the vague specific — milestones + check-in date
"Top of the band" The current structure limits the ask Ask about the next band — and the path to it
"Standard process" Easier to say no to you than to change the process Name your specific impact — above-standard work deserves above-standard consideration
"I'll think about it" Buying time — possibly to avoid the decision Lock in a specific follow-up date before you leave
Silence / no response They are processing — or they're waiting for you to fill the space Do NOT fill the silence. Wait. Your patience is power.

5 Rules for Handling Objections

1

Do not apologize for asking

Starting your response with "I'm sorry, I just thought..." undermines everything that follows. You made a well-researched ask. You have nothing to apologize for.

2

Do not accept vague on any objection

"Not ready yet" without milestones is a slow no. "Budget frozen" without a timeline is a permanent no. Convert every vague objection into something specific before the conversation ends.

3

Do not accept on the spot

You are allowed to say "I appreciate that — let me think about it and follow up." Taking time to consider a counter is professional, not weak.

4

Follow up in writing

After every negotiation conversation — regardless of outcome — send a brief email summarizing what was discussed and what was agreed. This protects you and creates accountability.

5

A flat "no" with no path forward is information

An organization that cannot provide a clear reason for declining a well-prepared, evidence-based ask — and offers no alternative path — is telling you something about the ceiling in that space. That information is valuable.
