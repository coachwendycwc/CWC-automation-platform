Compensation Research Guide

Know what the market says before you walk in the room. Data is confidence.

You cannot negotiate effectively with feelings alone. Market data is your anchor. It transforms your ask from personal preference into objective alignment. This guide walks you through where to look, what to look for, and how to synthesize your research into a number you can stand behind.

1

Glassdoor

glassdoor.com/Salaries
Free
Best for: Company-specific salary ranges by title and location. Particularly useful if you already work at or are interviewing with a specific company.
  • Search by exact job title AND location for the most accurate data
  • Filter by company size (small / mid / enterprise) to match your context
  • Look at both the "Base Pay" range and the "Additional Pay" (bonus, equity)
  • Read the salary reports alongside company reviews — culture context matters
2

LinkedIn Salary

linkedin.com/salary
Free (with Premium)
Best for: Industry-level benchmarks filtered by years of experience, education, and location. More granular than Glassdoor for professional roles.
  • Use the filters — years of experience is critical to isolate comparable data
  • Check median vs. 75th percentile — you want to understand what top performers make
  • Cross-reference with actual job postings to see what companies are actively paying
3

Levels.fyi

levels.fyi
Free Tech / Corporate
Best for: Tech and corporate roles with equity. Shows total compensation (base + bonus + equity) by company and level.
  • Particularly useful if your role includes significant equity (RSUs/options)
  • Compare "TC" (total comp) not just base salary — equity is often the biggest number
  • Pay attention to level designations — "L5 at Google" vs. "P5 at Meta" have equivalents
4

Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS)

bls.gov/oes
Free Government Data
Best for: Authoritative government data on occupational wages by metro area and industry. Useful as an authoritative third source to back up your research.
  • Use the Occupational Employment and Wage Statistics (OEWS) tool
  • Filter by your state and metro area for geographic accuracy
  • Government data is slower to update — supplement with real-time sources
5

Payscale

payscale.com
Free / Paid
Best for: Personalized salary reports that factor in your specific skills, certifications, and experience level.
  • The "What Am I Worth" tool is useful for custom salary reports
  • Input your full skill set — each additional skill can change the estimate
  • Use the market data as a range, not a single number
6

Your Network

Trusted colleagues, mentors, professional associations
Free
Best for: Real, current, context-specific data that no database can replicate. A trusted peer in the same function at a comparable company is worth three Glassdoor reports.
  • Ask: "Are you comfortable sharing your comp range or what you know the market is paying for [role]?"
  • Professional associations (SHRM, NBMBAA, Lean In Circles) often publish salary data
  • Executive coaches and recruiters are often the best sources — they see hundreds of offers
  • LinkedIn connections at comparable companies can be invaluable — especially other WOC
WOC Research Note

General salary databases often reflect historical wage gaps — which means the "median" may underrepresent your true market value. Supplement public data with network conversations, especially with women of color in comparable roles. They are your most accurate benchmark.

How to Synthesize Your Research

1

Pull data from at least 3 sources

Log each source's range in the worksheet below. The overlap between sources becomes your credible range.

2

Identify your "market range" — the band where 3+ sources agree

Don't average the numbers. Look for where sources converge. That convergence zone is your anchor.

3

Position yourself within the range based on your experience and impact

If you're delivering top-quartile results, your ask should be in the top quartile of the range — not the median.

4

Set your target at the top of your acceptable range

You will almost certainly be countered. Your first ask should be at the ceiling of what you'd accept, not the floor. Research shows specific asks (e.g., $127,500 instead of "around $125K") result in better outcomes.

5

Know your walk-away number

Before the conversation, decide the minimum you will accept. Anything below that is a signal to reconsider the opportunity.
