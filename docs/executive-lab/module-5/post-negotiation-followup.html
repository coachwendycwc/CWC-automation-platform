Send a Confirmation Email

Always put the agreement in writing — within 24 hours.

A brief follow-up email creates a written record that protects you. It is not aggressive — it is professional. Use the template below as your starting point and customize it with your own words.
Why This Matters for WOC

Verbal agreements can be forgotten or reframed. A written confirmation means there is a record your manager acknowledged. This is not distrust — it is professional documentation. Leaders in other demographics do this routinely.

Email Template — Customize Before Sending
To: [Manager Name]
Subject: Follow-Up — Our Conversation on [Date]
Hi [First Name],

Thank you for making time to speak with me on [Date]. I wanted to follow up in writing to confirm what we discussed.

As I understood it, we agreed to: [summarize the agreed outcome — be specific].

[Optional: The effective date for these changes will be ________.]

[Optional: We also discussed reconnecting on ________ to revisit ________.]

Please let me know if I've missed or mischaracterized anything. I'm looking forward to [next step / continued partnership].

Thank you,
[Your Name]

30-Day Follow-Through Checklist

What was promised must actually be delivered — track it.

Agreements sometimes stall after the conversation. This checklist is your accountability tool. Click each item as it's confirmed. If something isn't happening, you have documentation that it was promised.

If the Answer Was "No" or "Not Yet"

A no today is a negotiating position, not a verdict.

Complete this section if you did not receive what you asked for. A refusal is information. Use it to plan your next move with more precision.
Remember This

"No" often means "not the way you asked" or "not right now." WOC are twice as likely to accept an initial no as final — when it isn't. Your next move matters more than their first response.

Keep Building Your Case Year-Round

Your next negotiation starts the day after this one ends.

Top negotiators don't wait until review season to build their case. Set up the habits and systems now that make your next ask even stronger.
Module 5 Reminder

Advocacy is not a one-time event — it is a discipline. Every time you document a win, negotiate a boundary, or ask for what you have earned, you are building a skill that compounds over your career. The leaders who negotiate best are the ones who do it most consistently.
