Negotiation Scripts by Scenario

Four complete scripts for four common negotiation situations. Adapt to your voice — the structure is non-negotiable.

How to use these scripts: Yellow brackets are your fill-in fields — replace them with your specific numbers, titles, and wins. Read each script aloud at least twice before your conversation. The goal is not memorization — it is muscle memory. When you know the structure, the words come naturally.
1

Annual Review — Salary Increase Request

You're in your current role and it's review time (or you're requesting a review)

Annual Review
Opening the Conversation
"I appreciate you making time for this, [Name]. I've been thoughtful about this conversation, and I want to be direct: I'd like to discuss my compensation and make sure it reflects my contributions and aligns with the market."
— Pause. Let them respond. If they invite you to continue, move to the case.
Building the Case
"I've done some market research. Based on my role, my [X years] of experience, and the scope of my work in [location/industry], the market range for someone in my position is [$X to $Y]. My current compensation is [$Z], which is below that range."

"Beyond the market data, I want to name some specific contributions from this past year. I [Win #1 — with impact]. I also [Win #2 — with impact]. Those two things alone represent significant value to the organization."
Making the Ask
"Based on that, I'm asking for a base salary of [$specific number]. That would bring me to market and reflect the level I'm delivering at."

— Say the number. Stop talking. Wait.
Closing the Conversation
"I'd love to hear your thoughts. I'm committed to continuing to deliver at this level — I want to make sure my compensation reflects that commitment."
After they respond: If they need to check with someone or come back to you — ask for a specific timeline. "I appreciate that. Can we plan to reconnect by [specific date] with an answer?"
2

New Job Offer — Counteroffer Script

You have received an offer and you want to negotiate before accepting

New Offer
Initial Response to the Offer (Phone / Email)
"Thank you so much — I'm genuinely excited about this opportunity and [something specific about the role/team/mission]. I've received the offer and I'd like to take a day to review it fully. Is it okay if I follow up with you by [specific date]?"
— Always buy time. Never accept or counter on the spot.
Counteroffer Script (Call or Email)
"I've reviewed the offer carefully, and I want to reiterate how excited I am about this role. I'd like to share some context and make a counteroffer."

"Based on my research into market compensation for [title] in [location/industry], as well as my [X years] of experience and [specific expertise], I was expecting a base in the range of [$X to $Y]. The current offer of [$Z] is a bit below that."

"I'm asking for a base salary of [$specific number]. I'm confident that I'll bring that value and more from day one."
If They Say the Offer is Firm on Base
"I appreciate you being direct about the base. Can we talk about other elements of the package? I'm also interested in discussing [signing bonus / equity refresh / additional PTO / remote flexibility]. Is there flexibility there?"
WOC Note: Research shows women of color receive less initial offers than white counterparts for the same role. Your instinct may be to accept quickly to seem grateful. Resist. A well-delivered counteroffer signals confidence and market awareness — not ingratitude.
3

Requesting a Promotion

You want a title change — with or without an immediate compensation increase

Promotion
Opening the Conversation
"I want to have a direct conversation about my career path here and, specifically, about a promotion to [target title]. I've been thinking about the right time to bring this up, and I believe I've built a strong case."
The Case
"Here's what I've been doing at that level already: [2–3 specific examples of work beyond your current title]. I've also taken on [scope or leadership examples], which is typically associated with [target title] responsibilities."

"From a market standpoint, someone in a [target title] role with my scope of work typically earns between [$X and $Y]. My current compensation doesn't reflect the level I'm already operating at."
The Ask
"I'm asking to be formally promoted to [target title], with a corresponding compensation adjustment to [$specific number]. I believe that reflects the work I'm already delivering."
If They Say "Not Yet"
"I appreciate you being honest. Can you help me understand exactly what 'ready' looks like in your view? I'd like to have specific milestones so I can demonstrate that clearly — and can we agree on a 90-day check-in to revisit this?"
Practice prompt: Say your promotion ask out loud right now. Include your target title and specific number. Notice how it feels. That feeling is the exact discomfort you are building capacity to move through.
4

Negotiating Beyond Salary — Scope, Flexibility & Development

You want to negotiate non-cash elements of your package

Total Package
Scope Expansion Ask
"I want to talk about the scope of my role. I've been leading [project/function], which is [description of expanded scope]. I'd like to formally expand my responsibilities to include [specific area], and I'd like that to be reflected in my title and compensation."
Remote / Schedule Flexibility Ask
"I'd like to discuss the possibility of a [fully remote / hybrid / compressed schedule] arrangement. I've been delivering strong results — [specific example] — and I believe this flexibility would actually increase my productivity and long-term sustainability in this role. Here's what I'm proposing: [specific arrangement]."
Professional Development Budget Ask
"I'd like to invest in [specific certification / coaching / conference] this year, and I'm requesting [$specific amount] in professional development support from the organization. Here's how it connects to my work: [specific connection to role impact]. I see this as a return on investment for the team, not just for me personally."
Key principle: Non-cash asks should always be tied to organizational value — not just personal preference. Frame every ask as "here is how this benefits the team or the mission." It reframes the conversation from a favor to an investment.
