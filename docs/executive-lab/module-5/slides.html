The Silence Tax

Every year you don’t negotiate is a year of compounding loss. Un-asked-for raises, titles that don’t match your scope, and equity left on the table add up over a career to hundreds of thousands of dollars.

The Negotiation Penalty

Research shows WOC face more pushback, less goodwill, and harsher assessments when they negotiate the same things their white male peers negotiate routinely—and without consequence.

The Script Gap

Most women know they should ask. Very few know exactly what to say, how to frame it, or what to do when the first answer is no. That gap isn’t about confidence—it’s about preparation.

Know Your Worth  ·  Deep Dive

You Can’t Negotiate What You Haven’t Researched

Signs You’re Flying Blind

  • You don’t know your market rate
  • You’ve never asked peers what they earn
  • You’re guessing at the range in your field
  • You’re anchoring on your current salary instead of market data

The Breakthrough

  • Research 3 salary data sources (Levels.fyi, Glassdoor, LinkedIn, BLS)
  • Build a range: floor, target, and aspirational
  • Account for your geography, industry, and level
  • Add your total comp lens: equity, bonus, benefits, flexibility

The Negotiation Penalty  ·  Deep Dive

The System Is Designed to Make You Accept Less

The Likability Penalty

Women who negotiate are rated as less likable and less hireable by both male and female evaluators—a bias that intensifies for WOC, who already navigate likability penalties rooted in race.

The First Offer Trap

Organizations often open with the lowest defensible number. Accepting the first offer without negotiation is the single most expensive career mistake most women make.

Strategic Framing Works

Research shows that WOC who frame negotiation around collective benefit (“I want to continue contributing at a high level”) experience less penalty than those who use purely self-advocacy framing.

Module 5 Root Causes

Why Women of Color Don’t Ask—And Why That Needs to Change

Socialized to Be Grateful

“You should be glad to have a seat at the table.” This narrative is handed to WOC early and often—and it is costing you real money every single year.

No Role Models for This

If you’ve never seen someone who looks like you negotiate successfully, you have no script to follow. The absence of representation is a real skills barrier.

Fear of the Relationship

Worried that the ask will damage trust or make you seem ungrateful. But research is clear: most managers respect employees who know their worth and communicate it professionally.

Grateful Does Not Mean Aligned

You can be genuinely grateful for your role and still know that your compensation doesn’t match your contribution. Both things can be true. The ask is not a betrayal.

Waiting for Permission

Waiting for your manager to “notice” and reward you without a conversation is not a strategy. It is hope. And hope is not a career plan.

The Script Gap

Knowing you should ask and knowing what to say are two different things. This module closes that gap—permanently.

Module 5 Framework

The Four-Part Negotiation Script

Every effective negotiation conversation follows the same structure. This is yours.

The Four-Part Script

1. Open: “I’d like to have a conversation about my compensation. I’ve done my research and I believe there’s an opportunity for alignment.”

2. The Case: “Over the past [timeframe], I’ve [2–3 wins with metrics]. My work has directly contributed to [business outcome].”

3. The Ask: “Based on my track record and current market benchmarks of [$X–$Y], I’m requesting [specific number/title/scope].”

4. The Close: “I’m committed to this organization and want to continue growing here. How can we make this work?”

Strategy

Frame It Right. Change the Outcome.

How you position the ask matters as much as what you ask for. These framing strategies reduce the likability penalty without diluting the ask.

Lead with Contribution, Not Need

“Based on the impact I’ve delivered” lands better than “I need more money.” Anchor the ask to your value—not your circumstances.

Use Market Data as Cover

“My research shows the market range for this role is $X–$Y.” External data depersonalizes the conversation and makes the ask about facts, not emotions.

Name Your Commitment

“I’m invested in the work here and want to continue growing with this team.” This reduces the fear that the conversation signals flight risk.

Tool  ·  Objection Responses

Four Responses to the Four Things They Always Say

What They Say What You Say
“The budget is frozen.” “I understand. Can we agree on a number in principle and set a date for when the budget opens? I want to make sure we’re aligned on the path forward.”
“Let’s revisit next cycle.” “I’d like to do that. Can we schedule that conversation now and document what I need to accomplish to get there? I want to come back with clear evidence.”
“You’re already at the top of your band.” “That tells me the conversation may also be about a title and level change. I’d like to talk about what that path looks like and what the timeline is.”
“We give everyone the same increase.” “I appreciate that. I’d like to discuss a merit adjustment based on specific contributions outside of the standard cycle. Here’s what I’ve delivered…”

Beyond Salary

Negotiating Equity Is Where Wealth Happens

For many roles—especially in tech and high-growth companies—equity is where the real money is. Most women don’t negotiate it. That gap is generational.

What to Ask About Equity

What’s the vesting schedule? What’s the strike price vs. fair market value? Is there a refresh grant policy? Can I negotiate more equity in lieu of a higher base? What’s the liquidity timeline?

How to Frame It

“I’m very excited about this role. I’d like to explore whether there’s flexibility in the equity component. I’m thinking long-term here—can we talk about what a stronger equity package would look like?”

Remember: You can negotiate equity at a new offer, at annual review, and proactively after a major win. Refresh grants are standard at many companies—but only if you ask.

After the Conversation

The Ask Doesn’t End When the Meeting Does

If They Say Yes

Get it in writing. Send a follow-up email the same day: “Thank you for today’s conversation. I’m confirming our agreement to [specific terms] effective [date].” Paper trail is protection.

If They Say Not Yet

Get clarity on what “not yet” means. What do you need to accomplish? What is the timeline? Get milestones in writing. “Not yet” without a clear path is “no.”

If They Say No

This is data, not rejection. Ask what would need to change. Then decide: Is this an organization that values you? The answer to that question may be the most important one of your career.
